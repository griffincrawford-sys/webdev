History of Thanksgiving

History of Thnksgiving and how it was created

Thanksgiving originated from a 1621 harvest feast shared by the Plymouth colonists and the Wampanoag people, though days of thanksgiving predate this event. After various state and local proclamations, President Abraham Lincoln declared a national Thanksgiving Day in 1863, and it was officially set as the fourth Thursday of November by President Franklin D. Roosevelt in 1941.

Origins of the 1621 feast

  • The feast: The widely recognized "first Thanksgiving" was a three-day harvest feast in 1621 between English colonists and the Wampanoag tribe.
  • Purpose: It celebrated the colonists' successful first harvest after a brutal winter, and the Wampanoag contributed venison to the meal.
  • Food: The menu likely included fowl, venison, fish, shellfish, and vegetables, though modern Thanksgiving staples like turkey were not necessarily the main course.

    • Development into a national holiday

  • Colonial Thanksgivings: Throughout the 17th and 18th centuries, various colonies held their own days of thanksgiving, often tied to religious observances or military victories.
  • First national proclamation: In 1789, President George Washington issued the first national Thanksgiving proclamation, though it was not intended as an annual event.
  • Lincoln's role: In 1863, during the Civil War, Abraham Lincoln declared a national Thanksgiving Day to help unite the divided country.
  • Official date: The holiday was officially set to be celebrated on the fourth Thursday of November by President Franklin D. Roosevelt in 1941.
